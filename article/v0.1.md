## What This Is

This is the earliest working form of the article. The structure is loose. The argument is not yet fully developed. That is intentional.

The thesis:

> A picture can be worth more than the words that prompted it into existence — but only if it produces more understanding. The first diagram almost never does.

## The Core Problem

When we try to explain a system, we reach for a diagram too fast.

The diagram feels like progress. It looks like clarity. It has boxes and arrows and labels. It feels like we have moved from the fog of language into the precision of structure.

But the first diagram almost always reflects our assumptions, not the system. It maps what we think is happening, not what is actually happening. It organizes our confusion rather than dissolving it.

This is not a failure of diagramming. It is a failure of timing.

## ROY: Return on Your Words

ROY — Return on Your Words — is a way of measuring how much understanding a piece of communication actually produces relative to the effort it takes to produce and consume it.

A diagram that makes a complex system legible to someone who could not follow the prose version has a high ROY. A diagram that reproduces the author's mental model in visual form without adding clarity has a low ROY, regardless of how polished it looks.

The first diagram is usually a liar because it has high visual ROY and low actual ROY.

## Why Mermaid Changes This

Mermaid lowered the cost of diagramming to near zero.

When diagrams required design tools, time, and pixel-pushing, you only made one or two. You committed to them. You defended them. You did not iterate.

When diagrams are code — when you can generate, modify, and discard them in minutes — the cost of being wrong on the first try collapses. You can afford to let the first diagram be a liar. You just don't stop there.
