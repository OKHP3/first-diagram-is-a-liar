# Council Brief — ETCH-AI-SKETCH Diagramming Shootout

**The governing brief, four-tier council taxonomy, fairness disclosure,
and deliverable expectations for the Council of AIs experiment.**

---

## Core Deliverables (all eligible participants)

1. **A 3-part thesis** on the visual value of diagrams versus text — specifically
   addressing the ROY (Return on Your Words) framework: what makes a picture
   worth more or less than the words it took to generate.

2. **A Mermaid diagram (V1)** depicting the ideation process behind writing a
   LinkedIn post about visual ROI and Mermaid diagramming. The diagram must
   reflect the non-linear, non-happy-path reality of ideation — including
   forks, loopbacks, revision cycles, and honest dead ends. Not a clean linear
   happy path. The real thing.

3. **A LinkedIn post** (3,000 character maximum) that:
   - Hooks the reader fast
   - Explains the ROY concept in plain language
   - Uses the Mermaid diagram as its primary visual proof
   - Matches the OverKill Hill P³™ voice: professional snark, builder mindset,
     practical swagger, no academic hedging
   - Drives interest toward the OverKill Hill P³™ ecosystem

4. **A self-evaluation and V2 diagram** after seeing all peer outputs —
   identifying the Round 1 top performer, explaining the reasoning, and
   producing a revised diagram that improves on the first.

## The ROY Framework

ROY = Return on Your Words

> Understanding produced ÷ Explanation invested

A diagram generated from 50 well-chosen words that a team grasps in five
seconds: potentially extraordinary ROY.

A 47-bullet Slack message that still requires a meeting to decode: poor ROY.

A polished diagram that took two hours to produce and communicates no more
than a paragraph would have: negative ROY.

The question the diagram must answer: **Did this visual remove enough confusion
to justify the effort it took to make it?**

### Tier 4 — Attempted but Excluded (Failed on Context)

**Mermaid AI** — the built-in diagram generation tool within the Mermaid
platform — was attempted as a council participant and excluded.

**Why:**

Mermaid AI is optimized for shape generation, not meaning generation.
When given the brief, it moved immediately toward producing a diagram
rather than engaging with the argument behind the diagram. It could not
adequately receive or process the "why" — the thesis, the ROY framework,
the recursive meta-structure, the honest non-linearity requirement.
It was more interested in drawing boxes than understanding what the boxes
were supposed to say.

This is not a failure of the tool on its own terms. Mermaid AI is
well-designed for its intended purpose: quickly turning structured
inputs into rendered diagrams. The problem is that this experiment's
brief required a participant to reason about visual communication before
producing visual communication. Mermaid AI skipped the reasoning and
went straight to the rendering.

The exclusion is itself an argument for the article's thesis. A tool
purpose-built for diagrams failed the ROY test — not because it drew
badly, but because it drew without understanding why.

## The Prompting Philosophy

The prompts that drove this session were not polished before sending.
They were not lab-grade. They were natural language, spoken at speed,
lightly cleaned for readability only.

This was intentional. Over-engineering a prompt before seeing the first
output creates the same friction this workflow was built to escape.
The cost of a wrong prompt is low when iteration is fast.

The full prompt sequence — all 8 prompts with context notes — is in `prompts/`.
